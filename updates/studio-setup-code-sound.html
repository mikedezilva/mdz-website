The Studio Setup: Building IP Across Code and Sound

If you walk into my space, whether it is my home studio or the dedicated setup I run in Colombo, you will notice something pretty quickly. It is entirely driven by utility. I do not obsess over having the perfect, hyper-customized desk accessories. Everything in the room is designed around a single, very practical philosophy: I am here to build.

People often ask me how I balance being a technologist building digital infrastructure with being a musician and producer. They ask how I manage the transition from "coding mode" to "music mode."

The honest answer is that I don't look at it as two different modes. I just look at it as being a producer.

Whether I am writing software or writing music, I am trying to build my own IP. I constantly run into things I do not know how to do, and when that happens, I just have to learn what I need to move forward. The mindset is exactly the same, only the tools change.

The Workhorse over the Showpiece

It is easy to get caught up in feeling like you need the perfect rig to start creating. For a long time, I fell into that trap too. But the reality is that the core of my workflow today is just a laptop.

I don't use a massive studio desktop. The laptop is the workhorse that travels with me, running logic sessions and code editor environments. As for keyboards and mice, I just use whatever is lying around. Over the years I've realized that the magic doesn't live in high-end gear. It lives in putting in the hours, regardless of what hardware you are running.

Embracing the Weird

While my computer setup is mostly standard, my music rig is where I allow myself to experiment. But even here, it is not about building a pristine, expensive collection. It is about chasing sounds.

My current guitar rotation is lean. I use a classic Telecaster, an ESP LTD 7-string for heavier work, and a Cort which is my current favorite. I am not too big on building a massive guitar collection.

Instead, I play around with effects. A lot. My chain is usually a mix of analog synths and pedals like the Meris Hedra, Boss Slicer, Ibanez Echo Shifter, Boss synth pedals, JHS drives, and ZVEX boxes. I gravitate towards sounds that feel a bit unpredictable or weird, because that is usually where the most interesting ideas start.

The Process is the Same

When it comes to tracking, the workflow is streamlined. The signal path generally flows through my SSL gear, into the SSL SiX console, and finally into the DAW. If the idea is rooted in electronic music, I will open Ableton. But if it is live instrumentation (which is most of the time), Logic Pro is my go-to.

At the end of the day, a studio is just a room with tools in it. The SSL console, the pedals, the Mac, they are all just different hammers and chisels.

If you are struggling to start because your setup does not look like the ones on Instagram, remember that the real infrastructure is not physical. It is the mindset of recognizing that every line of code and every recorded audio track is just a building block. Start with whatever you have in front of you, and just focus on producing.
